SpreadY is a system consisting of 5 wireless light sticks.

It offers several operating modes that enable the therapist to stimulate voice, speech and language development, motor and social skills, spatial orientation, improved memory, concentration, attention, and basic cognitive and mathematical abilities in children. This system provides support to children with sensorimotor disorders and delays in psychomotor development, and helps them strengthen their communication skills and confidence.

The operating modes offered by this device are:

-   MICROPHONE
-   MEMORY
-   MANUAL

## **MICROPHONE**

The MICROPHONE mode displays a light reaction triggered by sound using a built-in microphone. In this mode, the sticks visually display sound propagation in space. Lights turn on and off linearly in a predetermined sequence: blue, green, yellow, red, gray.

This mode is suitable for situations when working on:

-   voice imposition,
-   vocalization,
-   controlling sound intensity, rhythm and duration,
-   recognizing and eliminating harmful voice use patterns (such as screaming and producing loud sounds while playing),
-   reduced voice endurance,
-   various forms of dysphonia,
-   attention disorders.

Technical Instructions: After turning on the sticks, open the app and enter the SpreadY device section when its symbol/photo lights up green. Then press the MODE button (in the lower right corner) and select MICROPHONE.

GAME SUGGESTIONS:

To start the game, the child needs to notice the connection between their voice and the light display on the sticks, which you can do in the following way: activate the sticks with your voice by simple prolonged vocalization such as "AAA" or "EEE". Then encourage the child to try the same thing by speaking or making a sound. In response to the produced sound, lights on the sticks will begin to turn on linearly in the sequence described above. We expect the child to notice how the lights respond to their voice.

### **What's Your Color**

Goal: color recognition and naming, ability to discriminate between colors.

Description: The therapist produces a sound (by vocalizing or singing a single tone for 1-2 seconds), while simultaneously lights on the sticks turn on in the same rhythm. The therapist asks the child to recognize and name the color of the base of the stick whose light turned on last (blue, red, yellow...).

### **Recognize Me**

Goal: symbol recognition and naming, ability to discriminate between symbols.

Description: For this game we use cards with certain symbols or pictures, inserted into holders previously placed on the sticks. The task is for the child to recognize and name the concepts found on the card whose light turned on last.

### **Light Me**

Goal: vocalization, acoustic exercise, voice control

Description: The task is for the child, at the therapist's request, to activate the light on that stick with their voice that contains a certain color or concept displayed on the card. Playing like this, the child observes their voice and becomes aware of its intensity, range, and audibility.

You can introduce new elements such as animal sounds - loudly mooing like a cow or quietly meowing like a cat, by placing cards with animal pictures on the sticks. The child's task is to activate, for example, the blue stick with the onomatopoeia of the animal shown.

### **Vowel Duration**

Goal: voice control and its duration.

Description: When the child vocalizes and activates lights on the sticks with their voice, we set them a new challenge – to control the length of their voice. When vocalization causes a light reaction, we count how many seconds they can sustain singing, for example, the vowel "E" (while we count to 2, 3, 4, 5...).
The task can also be a predetermined duration of vocalization, where the child themselves tracks and controls the conditions of the activity: lights on the stick, duration of the vowel, and in group work waiting for their turn.

In group work - organize children into two groups. The first group, together, with the same intensity and duration vocalization, activates one or more sticks. The task for the second group is to, together, imitate the set model with the same vocalization.

## **MEMORY**

In MEMORY mode, the light on the sticks turns on according to parameters previously set using the app.

Technical Instructions: After turning on the sticks, open the app and enter the SpreadY device section when its symbol/photo lights up green. Then press the MODE button (in the lower right corner) and select MEMORY.

In the app we set parameters for the maximum number of sticks that will turn on and the number of series - how many times that maximum set number will turn on. According to these parameters, the program turns on the sticks using a randomly generated pattern method. Using the app we can determine the time during which the sticks are on, as well as the time during which they are off between two series.

This mode is suitable for situations when working on the development of:

-   memory,
-   mathematical operations,
-   attention,
-   concentration,
-   fine motor skills,
-   bilateral coordination,
-   visual perception,
-   psychomotor re-education.

### **Remember Color and Concept**

Goal: developing attention and concentration through color and concept differentiation and naming

Description: Offer the child previously prepared cards in the colors of the stick bases. Then explain to them that some of the sticks will light up and turn off. Start with simple combinations that include only one series and a smaller number of sticks. The task is for the child to memorize which sticks were lit and to match cards in those colors in front of them according to that pattern.

Then gradually increase the task difficulty by adapting it to the cognitive capacity of the child you are working with. Through the app you can set from one to ten series of light displays and the number of sticks that will light up within one display.

This game can also be competitive. Divided into teams, children arrange cards according to the above principle. The winning team is the one that finishes first and has correctly guessed all the set color combinations in series.
Variation: you can ask the child to memorize the concepts on the cards placed on the sticks.

### **Addition**

Goal: practicing attention and concentration, practicing the mathematical operation of addition

Description: Explain to the child that some sticks will light up and turn off. The task can be for the child to add the total number of turned-on sticks, or to add only the number of turned-on sticks of one color (only blue, only red...).
Start with simple combinations that include a smaller number of series and turned-on sticks, then gradually increase the task difficulty by adapting it to the cognitive capacity of the child you are working with.

### **Color by Model**

Goal: development of fine motor skills, graphomotor skills and attention

Description: In front of the child are paper and colored pencils. Explain to the child that individual sticks will light up and they should try to remember them. Then activate the lights in memory mode. Start with simple combinations that include only one series and a smaller number of sticks, then gradually increase the task difficulty by adapting it to the child's abilities. When the sticks turn off, the child draws as many circles as there were lit sticks and colors them according to the set model.

### **Brain Gym**

Goal: development of fine motor skills, bilateral coordination, visual perception, attention, concentration

Description: arrange 5 sticks in front of the child and place cards on them (from appendix X). Each of the cards shows shapes: circle, square, rectangle, star, and "bunny ears".
Prepare paper for the child (from appendix) on which are circles on the left side in the colors of stick bases, and on the right shapes.
When the light turns on on one of the sticks, the game begins. The task is for the child to memorize which base color was activated and what shape is shown on the card. Then on the paper find a circle of the appropriate color and touch it with the left index finger, and the shape found on the card of the lit stick touch with the right index finger.

Variation: Arrange the sticks and cards as in the previous game. In this game, symbols represent a hand in different positions:

\- square - palm turned down with closed fingers
\- star - spread fingers,
\- rectangle - hand with extended fingers resting on the little finger and side of the palm,
\- circle - fist,
\- bunny ears - two fingers extended and spread, other fingers closed in a fist.

When the light turns on on one of the sticks, the game begins. The task is for the child to memorize which base color was activated and what shape is shown on the card. Then on the paper find a circle of the appropriate color and touch it with the left index finger. Form the right hand according to the corresponding shape shown on the card.

### **Catch the Color**

Technical Instructions: memory or manual

Goal: Visual perception and movement control, attention

Description: The child stands in front of the sticks and watches the lights that randomly alternate in one or more series. Agree in advance, according to the child's psychomotor abilities, for example, to snap fingers when the gray stick turns on, and tap knees when the yellow one lights up. When other sticks light up, observe without reacting with movement. If the child is successful, increase the requirements by introducing a new movement.

## **SPREADY MANUAL**

In MANUAL mode, the light on the sticks is turned on by the therapist using the app according to the exercises they want to apply in working with the child.

Technical Instructions: After turning on the sticks, open the app and enter the SpreadY device section when its symbol/photo lights up green. Then press the MODE button (in the lower right corner) and select MANUAL.

This mode is suitable for situations when working on the development of:

-   attention,
-   concentration,
-   visual perception,
-   fine motor skills,
-   bilateral coordination,
-   psychomotor re-education,
-   sequencing,
-   basics of mathematical operations,
-   memory,
-   analysis and synthesis of words and sentences
-   phonological awareness
-   thinking,
-   categorization
-   developing movement coordination,
-   balance,
-   development of social skills

### **Paths of the Day**

Goal: visual perception, sequencing - understanding parts of the day and their connection.

Description: We previously prepare pictures that represent parts of the day (morning, noon, afternoon, evening, night). After the child becomes familiar with the content of the pictures, we place them on the sticks in random order, in the number and complexity that corresponds to the child's abilities. The child should determine the correct order of the parts of the day and understand the concept and determine what comes before and what comes after in the day. As confirmation of the correct answer, the child receives a light signal.

Adjustment:

In this way we can play any game that requires the child to recognize the sequence of events (seasons, stories in pictures, human life cycle, plants or animals, what is made from what...).

For each correct answer, the therapist activates the light on the stick as confirmation.

For children who have developed speech-language skills, additional questions are desirable such as: "What do we do in the morning?", "When do we go to sleep?" or to arrange similar pictures in the correct sequence where activities we do in a certain part of the day, and then verbalize them to others.

### **From Voice to Word**

Goal: developing phonological awareness, word analysis and synthesis

Description: For word analysis practice, select the "One" button in the app, because this way the lights on the sticks turn on one by one. Cards with letters that make up the word we want to analyze are correctly placed on the sticks. One stick represents one letter. The exercise begins with the therapist saying the word (or showing the child a picture where, for example, the sun is) and asking the child to divide it into sounds. The task of the game is for the child to say one sound at a time represented by the symbol on the card, and the therapist sequentially turns on the sticks when the child correctly says the sounds in order.

For word synthesis practice, select the "Any" button in the app, because the sticks then turn on one by one and remain on until the end of the exercise. The placement of cards and the beginning of the exercise are the same as in the previous case. You practice word synthesis by having the child sing one sound at a time. At the other transition of when the therapist turns on the light on the next stick and so on until they sing all the sounds of the given word. The duration of singing an individual sound follows the length of the stick's light.
After all the sounds, the child says the word they recognized or draws the concept it represents on paper.

Variations: You can upgrade and modify the game by:

-   breaking the word down into syllables or combining syllables into words (first shorter, then longer) and thus developing early reading.
-   asking questions like: Which sound do you hear at the beginning? Which at the end?
-   giving a new word and asking the child to replace the first sound in the word (for example, dog, the child says a new word cat), to practice sound discrimination
-   if the child recognizes graphemes, arrange cards with corresponding letters on the sticks, until you assemble the given word.

### **Build/Take Apart Word**

Goal: word analysis and synthesis

Description: We prepare several cards that are arranged in front of the child. They show different pictures (flower, tree, bird, sun). On the sticks are arranged letters that together designate one of the offered words (by the principle one stick - one letter: f-l-o-w-e-r). Cards with letters are placed on the sticks in random order. The task is for the child to assemble the word from the offered letters that designates the name of the picture from one of the offered cards (flower). For word synthesis practice, select the "Any" button in the app.
The child says the sound or shows letters in the correct order, and the therapist follows and turns on the light to confirm the correct answer. When they assemble the entire word, the child says it, and all sticks remain on.
The next step is a game without cards. When the child masters the rules of this game, they need to assemble the word only based on the offered letters.
Adjustment: This principle can serve both for word analysis and synthesis. It can also be organized as syllable reading that children combine into words, and then words into sentences. In this way we stimulate early reading in children.

### **Building Sentences**

Goal: sentence building, semantic enrichment, developing sentence structure

Description: We prepare cards with symbols on the principle of 1 card - 1 word and place them in the correct order on the stick. We build sentences from 2, 3, or more words. We encourage the child to speak words in order with the help of cards.

Adjustment: We adapt the number of words to the child's abilities. We can leave the sticks on or turn on only one. We can help the child by previously turning on the stick that follows.

Variation 1: We prepare cards with symbols on the principle of 1 card - 1 word and place them on the sticks in random order. The task is for the child to speak the words of the concepts on the pictures in the correct order and build simple sentences from 2, 3, or more words that are meaningful and correct (for example, The girl draws a flower).

Variation 2: When children master the game, you can make it more difficult by (in MEMORY mode) randomly activating sticks and thus composing a sentence that is semantically incorrect (for example, "Flower girl draws.") The task is for the child to notice the error and form a grammatically correct sentence.

Note: you can apply this game to children with typical development, for whom such a word game will not lead to adopting the wrong model.

### **My Finger, Your Stick**

Goal: finger differentiation, visual perception, attention

Description: Place 5 sticks on a table or floor, one next to the other. Each stick represents one finger (first stick – thumb, second stick – index finger and so on). The child sits opposite the sticks and holds hands on the table or floor. They need to raise the finger that is in the same position as the stick that lights up (for example, the fourth stick - ring finger).

Adjustment: After the child gets comfortable with the basic steps, you can gradually increase the challenge by:

-   activating multiple sticks at the same time (where the child needs to raise multiple fingers);
-   asking the child to raise fingers correctly in different conditions (for example, when hands are in the air without support, etc.);
-   adding tasks like: "Raise the finger whose stick turned on first/last.";
-   incorporating other skills, such as recognizing ordinal numbers from 1 to 5. The child first follows the verbal instruction "Raise your fifth finger", when the child guesses we turn on the light as confirmation of the correct answer.

Note:
Adjustment is assessed by the therapist. This game is designed to help the child recognize, distinguish, and correctly name their fingers, develop awareness of their position and precision in differentiation.

By practicing this way, the child strengthens fine motor skills, develops eye-hand coordination, concentration, and the ability to direct attention to one specific task (finger recognition and connection with a light signal).

These games, according to the set tasks, can be played in "Manual" mode or in "Memory" mode.

### **What It Belongs To**

Goal: development of visual perception, attention and memory

Description: For this game we need pictures with related meanings (for example, umbrella and cloud with rain; spoon and plate...), where one from each pair is on the stick, and the rest are mixed in front of the child. The child picks a picture and matches it with the one on the stick. For each correct solution, the child receives a light signal as confirmation.

### **Ball Under Feet**

Goal: Developing movement coordination, balance, concentration, stimulating cognitive abilities, developing social skills in group games.

Description of activity:
Sticks are arranged in a semicircle on the floor around the child, at a sufficient distance that they can extend their legs to them. The child sits opposite the sticks in a central position. They hold a small ball with their feet that they can easily manipulate. When the stick lights up, the child rolls the ball with their feet towards it as much as they can. They simultaneously control leg movements and coordinate so that the ball does not escape. Then they return it along the same principle.
After that, they watch which stick will light up next and repeat the movements to each next stick. For this exercise, select the "One" button in the app.

The child can face additional challenges by:

-   each stick being marked with cards with different concepts that the child will say while rolling the ball towards them;
-   moving in different ways to the sticks (walking, walking backwards, hopping on one leg, running) and returning to the starting position. In this case, adjust the distance to the method of movement;
-   changing the direction of movement and thus additionally practicing coordination and balance;
-   rolling the ball while moving, which promotes even better control and fine motor skills;
-   saying numbers, letters of the alphabet or months of the year while moving towards the sticks (which helps them understand sequence and order);
-   using larger balls that are easier to roll;
-   crossing set obstacles.

### **Follow the Sequence**

Goal: Developing logical thinking and memory, mastering concepts and their connection in a logical sequence, developing movement coordination, concentration, spatial orientation.

Description: Arrange the sticks linearly at a distance that allows the child to move freely. Previously we talk with the child about the days of the week, which are working days, and what is the weekend. We establish what are the child's weekly routines (Wednesday and Friday they come to the speech-language pathologist, Monday and Thursday they do sports, which days they go to kindergarten, to grandma, etc.). After that, the child draws or finds ready-made cards that they can connect with their daily routines and hang them on sticks. The child needs to move from one stick to another and while doing so, say the concepts that follow a logical sequence, and the pictures help them remember the days of the week. For each correct answer, the therapist turns on the stick.

Adjustment: During the game we assess the abilities of the children and adapt the requirements accordingly. We change the ways of moving from one to another stick, the speed of movement, the distance between sticks, the inclusion of additional visual stimuli or different props that can make the child's movement more difficult (lightly rolling a ball on the floor).

### **Touch the Circle**

Goal: midline crossing

Description: Previously prepare 5 circles in stick colors and stick them on a wall at a height adjusted to the child. The child stands with their back to the wall and holds a ball with both hands. Opposite them are sticks whose lights randomly turn on and signal for them to react. After identifying the color, the child turns toward the wall (without moving their feet), aims the ball at the circle of the appropriate color and touches it. After that, they return to the starting position ready for the next task.
